Hike the Hike

On why the journey deserves more of your attention than the destination.

Hong Ting  —  April 2026

I remember hiking Kota Kinabalu and telling myself: don't linger. The faster you get to the top, the longer you get to enjoy the view from the peak. The summit was the point. Everything below it was just getting there.

That logic made complete sense to me at the time. Growing up, "focus" and "don't get distracted" were among my parents' most repeated refrains, and honestly, they weren't wrong. Tunnel vision, applied well, gets results.

I carried this into everything. When I played Pokémon, I had my six end-game team locked in from the start and trained them ruthlessly. Fun side routes, interesting detours, the quirky little Pokémon the game was quietly inviting me to explore... I largely ignored them. Eyes on the prize.

But I'm starting to think this is a bad trade.

We optimise our lives around the 1%.

Here's the uncomfortable maths: reaching the top of a hike earns you maybe five to ten minutes at the peak. And if you'd pushed a little harder, skipped a few rest stops, powered through the scenic bits? Maybe you get there ten minutes earlier. Ten extra minutes at the summit, in exchange for an entire hike spent with your head down. A promotion or a good grade gives you a day, maybe two, of that quiet glow. These are the moments we organise our lives around. And yet the time actually spent working toward them, the grinding, the training, the ordinary Tuesday afternoons, that's the other 99%. It feels strange to spend so much energy optimising for the 1% while treating everything else as a cost to be minimised.

There's a hike I keep coming back to in my mind: Hooker Valley in New Zealand. My favourite hike in the world, and not because of the destination. It's because of what it felt like to simply be in it. The cold air, the sound of the rivers, the way the mountains sat around you like they'd always been there. Every step felt like something worth having, not just a step closer to the end. And yet I still knew where I was going. I wasn't wandering aimlessly. I was moving toward something, and I was fully present in the moving.

Same trail. Different experience entirely.

That's the balance I'm chasing now. Direction without fixation. Knowing where you're headed, while actually showing up for the walk.

Sometimes that even means taking the unplanned turn. The detour that doesn't fit neatly into the plan but ends up being the most memorable part of the trip. But that's a story for another time.

If life is a hike, the summit is just one moment. We spend almost everything on the trail, in the in-between, in the steps that don't make for great photos but make up the bulk of a life. The question isn't whether to have a destination. Direction matters. The question is whether you're present for the hike itself.

Don't just hike to the top. Hike the hike.
