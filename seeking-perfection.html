On Growth — Essay No. 2

Seeking Perfection Only Works
If You Have One Thing to Love

The Kobe mentality is real. It's just not designed for a full life.

The Missing Class — 6 min read

For a long time, I wanted to win at everything. Not in a casual way. I mean the kind of wanting where losing made me physically uncomfortable — where I'd go home and practise whatever I'd just failed at until my hands hurt or my eyes burned. I hated losing. I hated the feeling of someone being better than me at something I cared about.

My idols were Kobe and Jordan. If you know anything about them, you know that obsession isn't a strong enough word. These were men who chose basketball so completely that everything else — relationships, rest, leisure, balance — became secondary. And it worked. They became the greatest to ever do it.

I thought that was the model. Go all in. Care about winning more than the next person. Let nothing distract you.

Then life handed me things I also cared about.

The problem with all-in

The Kobe model works. But it has a condition that nobody talks about: it only works if there is genuinely one thing you want to be great at. One arena. One pursuit. Everything else subordinated to it without regret.

Most of us don't live like that. We have people we love. Work we care about. Health we want to maintain. Friendships we want to keep. Hobbies that make us feel like ourselves. Trying to apply the Kobe mentality across all of those doesn't produce five Kobe-level lives. It produces five half-lived ones.

Mastery and breadth are both valid. The mistake is applying the wrong model to your life.

What I had to unlearn

I had to stop measuring myself against people who had made completely different trade-offs than me. Kobe chose basketball over everything. That's a valid choice — and a costly one, in ways that only became visible later in his life. When I was admiring his obsession, I wasn't accounting for what it cost him.

The shift I had to make was from depth as the only measure of worth, to depth as one valid way of living — alongside breadth, which is another.

Breadth isn't the consolation prize for people who couldn't commit. It's a different kind of richness.

Someone who is a decent parent, a capable professional, a loyal friend, a curious learner — that person is not a failure at any of those things. They're something harder to name but deeply worth being: whole.

Lifelong learning, not lifelong mastery

The reframe that helped me most was moving from mastery as the goal to learning as the practice. Mastery is a destination — you arrive, you've made it, what now? Learning is a posture. You stay curious, you keep improving, you measure yourself against who you were last year rather than against some ideal of perfection.

The goal moves from destination to direction.

This doesn't mean going easy on yourself. It doesn't mean lowering standards or settling. It means competing with the right opponent — which is the version of you that existed before. It means recognising that a life of breadth has its own kind of excellence, just distributed differently.

I still push hard. I still hate coasting. But I've stopped treating every domain of my life as a competition I'm losing. Instead, I ask: am I better than I was? Am I learning? Am I showing up?

That's enough. And honestly, it's harder than just winning.
