On Growth — Essay No. 2

Jack of All Trades, Master of None —
But Oftentimes Just Maybe Better Than Master of One

The phrase was never an insult. Someone added that part later.

The Missing Class — 7 min read

Here is a quote you think you know: "Jack of all trades, master of none."

You've probably heard it used as a gentle put-down, a polite way of saying someone is too scattered, too unfocused, too unwilling to commit. A consolation prize for people who couldn't pick a lane.

You may also have heard a version that adds a redemptive second half: "but oftentimes better than a master of one." It gets attributed to Shakespeare online, shared as a feel-good corrective to the insult. The problem is that attribution is almost certainly false. Shakespeare's works are fully searchable, and that line doesn't appear anywhere in them. The "full quote" is likely something someone invented and the internet ran with.

But here's what's actually true, and more interesting: the original phrase just "Jack of all trades" dates to the early 1600s, and it contained no insult at all. It was simply a description of someone capable across many domains. A compliment. The "master of none" part wasn't added until 1785, by which point society had started to fetishise specialisation. Someone took a phrase that celebrated breadth and quietly appended a criticism to it.

The insult was the addition. The original was fine.

I find that genuinely clarifying and it's a good frame for everything that follows.

The idols I chose

Growing up, my idols were Michael Jordan and Kobe Bryant. Not just for the trophies, but for the way they wanted it. The obsession. The refusal to accept second place. The stories of Jordan inventing slights from opponents just to fuel his drive, Kobe arriving at the gym before dawn and leaving after dark. These were men who had decided that one thing, basketball, was worth everything, and they gave it everything in return.

I absorbed this deeply. And for a long time, I tried to live it.

The sore loser who found his edge

I was, by any honest measure, a terrible loser. Not quietly. I mean the kind of losing where you feel it physically, a hot frustration rising in your chest that you can't quite name. It didn't matter what it was: table tennis, football, badminton, chess, Reversi. Losing felt like an accusation. Like the result was saying something about who I was.

But somewhere along the way, I figured out how to use it. Instead of just stewing in the feeling, I started doing something with it. I'd go home and study. Watch YouTube videos on technique, strategy, positioning. Train quietly, alone, and come back better. The rage became fuel. The loss became a to-do list.

I was lucky to grow up with the internet, that sudden, almost magical access to information that my parents' generation never had. Whatever I wanted to get better at, there was someone online who had already broken it down. I just had to be willing to put in the hours.

The shift wasn't about caring less. It was about turning loss into a loop that goes somewhere.

The belief I still hold

Out of all of this came a conviction I still carry today, and that some people find uncomfortable when I say it out loud: I genuinely believe that with enough focused time and effort, I could reach 80% of the ability of almost anyone in almost any field.

Not an Olympian. I understand the fine margins at the very top, the fractions of a percent that separate world-class from merely excellent. But a strong amateur? A competent professional? Someone in the top fifth of any given pursuit? Yes. I believe that's available to most people who are willing to put in the work. The internet made information cheap. Deliberate practice works. The gap between "talented" and "trained" is smaller than we think.

Hard work won't make you the best in the world at something. But it will take you further than most people are willing to go.

The question I had to eventually face wasn't whether this belief was true. It was whether acting on it, everywhere and all the time, was actually how I wanted to live.

The problem with applying it to everything

As I got older, my life got fuller. Not just with interests, but with things that genuinely mattered: relationships, work, health, the slow-building projects that don't give you a score or a leaderboard. And I started to notice something.

The Jordan-Kobe model has a hidden requirement: it only works when you have one thing. One arena. One pursuit that everything else orbits around. Jordan had basketball. Kobe had basketball. They made sacrifices in other parts of their lives that most people, when they're honest about it, aren't actually willing to make. And perhaps shouldn't have to.

Most of us don't have one thing. We have many things we care about. And the scarcest resource in life isn't talent, or information, or even opportunity. It's time. You cannot pour 100% of yourself into everything that matters to you. The maths simply don't work.

The dashed line shows where Kobe's basketball peak sits. The balanced model doesn't reach it but nothing is left at zero.

The reframe that changed things

The shift for me wasn't about lowering my standards. It was about realising I had been measuring myself against the wrong kind of excellence.

Kobe's excellence was vertical, one pursuit, taken as deep as humanly possible. What I was slowly learning to value was something more horizontal: the ability to show up well across multiple dimensions of a life. To be a decent human, a capable professional, a present friend, a curious learner, not world-class at any of them, but genuinely engaged with all of them.

And then I found the reframe that made it click for me. Think about Pokémon. The joy of the game is in the hunt. The next undiscovered creature, the next type you haven't encountered, the sense that the world still has something to show you. Players who reach 100% completion often report the same thing: once the Pokédex is full, the game loses its pull. There's nothing left to find.

Mastery is completing the Pokédex. It's a worthy goal, but it ends the game. Lifelong learning keeps it going.

The mastery climber reaches the summit and stops. The lifelong learner is always somewhere on the slope and glad of it.

What I carry forward

I still believe in the 80% rule. I still think hard work takes you further than people expect, and I still feel that competitive flicker when I lose at something I care about. Some things don't change, and I'm not sure they should.

But I've stopped treating every domain of my life as a competition I'm losing. I've stopped measuring myself against people who made completely different trade-offs than me. Jordan gave up a version of a balanced life to be the greatest basketball player who ever lived. That was his choice, and it was a valid one. It just isn't mine.

My model now is less about a single idol and more about a kind of person: someone who moves through life with genuine curiosity, who keeps getting incrementally better at the things that matter, who doesn't need to be the best in the room to find meaning in the work.

Not a master of one thing. A student of everything. And honestly, that's a more interesting game to play.

Jack of all trades, master of none. The original version of that phrase asked nothing more of you than to be capable, curious, and broad. Someone added the insult centuries later. I think it's time we dropped it.
