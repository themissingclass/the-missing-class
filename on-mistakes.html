On Being Human

Grey is the Colour of Life

On mistakes, imperfection, and why neither defines you the way you think.

Hong Ting  —  April 2026  —  Essay No. 1

White is a fascinating colour, mostly because of how unforgiving it is. Anyone who has experienced the quiet dread of washing a white shirt knows this well. One stray drop from a darker garment and the shirt is, by most accounts, ruined. It almost feels inevitable. The probability of a white shirt staying white, over any meaningful stretch of time, is probably lower than the probability of it being stained.

And yet, in life, we often chase exactly that. A spotless record, a mistake-free existence, a version of ourselves without blemish or rough edge.

Here's the thing though. If you've ever met someone who seems truly perfect, no red flags, no visible flaws, no messy history, the most logical response isn't admiration. It's suspicion. Because no one leads a perfectly white life. Not a single human being. Somewhere along the way, drops of black find their way in. They always do. And when they do, something fundamental shifts. What was once an absolute has now moved into the realm of spectrum. And that realm, it turns out, is far more accurate to what life actually is. Shades of grey.

Nobody lives at the extremes. The question is where you sit, and how you move.

That's what makes us human, actually. A robot doesn't make mistakes. It executes. It optimises. It produces the same output under the same conditions, every time. But humans are not built for that. We misread situations, we act on incomplete information, we say the wrong thing at the right moment and the right thing at the wrong one. We are, by design, fallible. And that fallibility isn't a flaw in the system. It is the system.

The mistakes we make make us exactly who we are. Human.

I know this not just as an idea, but as something I have lived. There was a period in my life where I made mistakes so big and so profound that I was left genuinely questioning what kind of person I was. Mistakes that hurt people I cared about. Mistakes I felt powerless to reverse, and for a long time, could not even fully understand. It was a dark and disorienting place to be. What slowly became clear, after a long time sitting with it, was that what I had to accept wasn't who I had become. It was what I had done. Those are two very different things, and learning to tell them apart was everything.

That mistake almost broke me. But it also taught me something I couldn't have learned any other way. That if life is going to throw us a few drops of black along the way, and it will, then the real work isn't in preventing the stain. It's in learning how to carry it. To learn from it. To make the best of what remains. To survive with the grey, and in time, hopefully thrive in it.

So if pure white isn't a realistic destination, maybe the better question is: where do you want to sit on the spectrum?

Because grey isn't a failure state. Grey is a scale, a range, a lived-in colour. And once you start thinking in shades rather than absolutes, something quietly shifts. You develop more humility toward others, because you start to see that the person you are judging isn't so different from you. They're just a different shade. You make room for the idea that good people can do bad things and still be worth something. That a single stain doesn't rewrite the whole shirt.

Once tainted, you can never be fully white again. At least not in one lifetime. There isn't enough time, or enough white paint, to completely drown out what came before. But maybe that was never the point.

Maybe life isn't about chasing the white.

Maybe it's about learning to live well in the grey.

After all, grey is the colour of life anyway.
