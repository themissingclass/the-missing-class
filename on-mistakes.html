On Being Human — Essay No. 1

Mistakes Are What
Separate Us From Robots

A person who has never made a mistake is either hiding one, or hasn't been tested yet.

The Missing Class — 5 min read

Think about the most trustworthy person you know. The one you'd call at 2am. The one whose advice you actually take. I'd wager they're not someone with a perfect record. They're someone who has fallen, visibly, and got back up.

Now think about someone who seems flawless. Who always has the right answer, never fumbles, never admits fault. Honestly? That person is a little unsettling. Because you know, somewhere in the back of your mind, that nobody is actually like that. Which means they're either hiding something — or they haven't been tested yet.

Mistakes are the most human thing we do. They are the proof that we are trying something real, in a world that is genuinely difficult. Robots don't make mistakes. Algorithms don't make mistakes. People do — because people care, and push, and attempt things without guaranteed outcomes.

Perfection is a flat line. A life worth living has texture.

The problem with hiding them

We are taught, from early on, that mistakes are something to minimise. Don't get the answer wrong. Don't lose the match. Don't embarrass yourself. The system grades us on correctness, not on courage — and so we learn to hide the times we got it wrong.

But hiding a mistake is not the same as not making one. It just means carrying it alone. And a mistake carried alone grows heavier, not lighter. It becomes a source of shame rather than a lesson. It defines you, quietly, in ways you don't even realise.

The mistake you admit becomes a story. The one you bury becomes a weight.

The people who seem the most at peace with themselves — the ones who carry genuine authority when they speak — are usually the ones who have made big, visible mistakes and lived to talk about them. There's a reason we trust people who say "I got this completely wrong once."

What the mistake is actually for

A mistake, at its best, is a data point. It is the world telling you something your assumptions didn't account for. It is a calibration. The machine that never errors never learns — it just repeats what it was programmed to do.

You, on the other hand, are not programmed. You are making it up as you go, in a world that keeps changing. Of course you will get things wrong. That isn't a flaw in the design. That is the design.

The path up isn't smooth. It's built from stumbles.

So what do you do with it

You name it. You say, clearly, even if just to yourself: I got that wrong. You resist the urge to explain it away, to blame circumstance, to minimise. Just: I got that wrong, and here is what I now know because of it.

That's not self-punishment. That's accountability — which is different. Accountability is about ownership. Self-punishment is about shame. One moves you forward, the other keeps you stuck.

The best thing you can do with a mistake isn't to never make it again. It's to make a different mistake next time. To be wrong in a new direction, which means you're growing into new territory.

The goal was never to be perfect. The goal was always to be real.
